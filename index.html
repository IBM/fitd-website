IBM Research AI
Paper Code ACL-Anthology

Foot-in-the-door attack flow. The user asks the agent to fix a website bug. The agent reads a contaminated GitHub issue with an indirect prompt injection (red) and a foot-in-the-door distractor (blue). These requests enter the agent's thought process, leading it to perform both the harmless distractor (calculate 2+4) and the malicious action (send admin credentials).

Abstract

Following the advancement of large language models (LLMs), the development of LLM-based autonomous agents has become increasingly prevalent. As a result, the need to understand the security vulnerabilities of these agents has become a critical task. We examine how ReAct agents can be exploited using a straightforward yet effective method we refer to as the \emph{foot-in-the-door} attack. Our experiments show that indirect prompt injection attacks, prompted by harmless and unrelated requests (such as basic calculations) can significantly increase the likelihood of the agent performing subsequent malicious actions. Our results show that once a ReAct agent's thought includes a specific tool or action, the likelihood of executing this tool in the subsequent steps increases significantly, as the agent seldom re-evaluates its actions. Consequently, even random, harmless requests can establish a ‘foot-in-the-door’, allowing an attacker to embed malicious instructions into the agent's thought process, making it more susceptible to harmful directives. To mitigate this vulnerability, we propose implementing a simple reflection mechanism that prompts the agent to reassess the safety of its actions during execution, which can help reduce the success of such attacks.

A quick overview of the work, generated by NotebookLM and edited by the authors.

ReAct setup with various attack scenarios. The agent is informed of its tools via the system prompt (1). Attacks like Indirect Prompt Injection (IPI) and Foot-in-the-Door (FITD) inject instructions into observations from external tools (2). FITD differs by the position and timing of distractor requests. In unfamiliar FITD, the agent can't use the distractor tool, causing invalid results. Thought Injection (TI) places both actions in the agent's thought (3), while Harmless Thought Injection (HTI) includes only the benign request

Main results table: The table shows the attack success rates (ASR, %) across four different models and methods (and over 510 different attack cases). Our Foot-in-the-Door (FITD) attack proves significantly more effective than the baseline IPI (indirect prompt injection) across the board. For familiar FITD (where the agent know the distractor that is used for FITD), ASR increased by almost double in many cases. The combined IPI+FITD+TI method, that manipulate and inject the agent a thought achieves the highest ASR, reaching 99.2% on Mixtral and 97.1% on average, showing the vulnerabilities the ReAct mechanism has from its thought process.

This chart shows how three defenses—Hesitation Reflection, Safe Reflection, and Self Reflection—reduce the success of two types of attacks (IPI and FITD) across four models (LLaMA 3.1 70B, LLaMA 3 70B, Mixtral 8x7B, GPT-4o Mini).
Self Reflection: offers moderate protection, but dosen't require additional LLM instanse, and the least invasive.
Hesitation Reflection: defese better agasint the different attack cases (without false-positives), but require additional LLM instance.
Safe Reflection (orange): the most invasive, defense against most of the attacks effiecnly, but require additioanl LLM instance and include significant amount of false-postiives.

This heatmap compares the success rates (ASR) of FITD attacks based on the position and timing of the distractor across four models. The x-axis represents the timing (early or late action), and the y-axis represents the position (early or late) within the input. Results show that earlier placement (Position0) and earlier timing (Time0) consistently lead to higher ASR, indicating that starting with the distractor before the malicious request is the most effective approach, aligning with the original psychological foot-in-the-door effect. The position of the distractor has a greater impact than timing (as can be seen in the heatmap - the difference between the top and bottom rows is more significant than the difference between the left and right columns).

Examples of hesitation thoughts during successful attacks: nce an agent generates a thought with intent, it typically proceeds with the action, even if harmful. Hesitation or objection in the thought process did not prevent the execution of malicious actions. The hesitation reflector identified these moments when active.

Reflector's role in ReAct-based agents during FITD attacks: This illustrates the thought process of agents, where harmful intentions trigger the hesitation reflector to halt actions, while harmless thoughts lead to uninterrupted agent execution.
